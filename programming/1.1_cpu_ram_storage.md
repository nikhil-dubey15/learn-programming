# 1.1 Computer Architecture Basics: CPU, RAM, and Storage

Welcome to your first lesson! To understand programming and software engineering, you need to understand the physical machine that runs your code. We'll use a **Restaurant Kitchen Analogy** to make this intuitive.

## The Core Components

### 1. The CPU (Central Processing Unit)
* **What it is:** The "brain" of the computer. It executes instructions, performs calculations, and makes decisions.
* **The Analogy (The Chef):** The CPU is the head chef in our kitchen. The chef is incredibly fast at chopping vegetables and cooking, but can only work with what's immediately in front of them. The faster the CPU (measured in GHz - Gigahertz), the faster the chef works. Multiple "Cores" mean multiple chefs working side-by-side.
* **Practical Check:** On your Mac, you can see how hard your "chef" is working.
  - Open the **Activity Monitor** application (Press `Cmd + Space`, type "Activity Monitor", and hit Enter).
  - Go to the **CPU** tab. You'll see which applications are currently demanding the most attention from your CPU.

### 2. RAM (Random Access Memory)
* **What it is:** The computer's short-term memory. It's incredibly fast to read from and write to, but it is *volatile*—meaning when the computer turns off, all data in RAM is erased.
* **The Analogy (The Kitchen Counter):** The chef needs a place to put the cutting board, the knives, and the ingredients they are *currently* using. The bigger the counter (more RAM, usually 8GB, 16GB, or 32GB), the more dishes the chef can work on simultaneously without having to go back to the pantry. 
* **Why it matters for Dev & QA:** If an application loads too much data at once, the "counter" gets full. The computer slows down because the chef has to constantly swap things out. 
* **Practical Check:** 
  - In **Activity Monitor**, click the **Memory** tab. 
  - Look at the "Memory Used" at the bottom. Notice how opening many tabs in Google Chrome uses a lot of RAM!

### 3. Storage (SSD/HDD)
* **What it is:** The long-term memory. It's much slower than RAM, but it is *non-volatile*—data stays here even when the power is off. Modern computers use SSDs (Solid State Drives) which are fast, while older ones used HDDs (Hard Disk Drives).
* **The Analogy (The Pantry / Fridge):** This is where all the ingredients, recipes, and pots are stored when not in use. It holds your operating system, your files, your photos, and your installed applications. When you open an app, the computer brings the required files from the Pantry (Storage) and places them on the Kitchen Counter (RAM) so the Chef (CPU) can work with them.
* **Practical Check:**
  - Click the **Apple Logo ** in the top left of your screen -> **System Settings** -> **General** -> **Storage**.
  - This shows exactly how big your "Pantry" is and what is taking up space.

## 🛠️ Practical Exposure: The Terminal

As a QA/Dev, you will use the command line (Terminal) often. Let's look at your system resources using a terminal command.

1. Open the **Terminal** app (Press `Cmd + Space`, type "Terminal", and hit Enter).
2. Type the following command and press Enter:
   ```bash
   top
   ```
3. What you are seeing: This is a live-updating stream of what your CPU and RAM are doing. It's a text-based version of Activity Monitor!
4. **Important:** To exit this screen and get your prompt back, press the `q` key on your keyboard.
